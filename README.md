# THE DISTINCTIVENESS GRADIENT
### A First-Principles Field Theory of Identity-Driven Aesthetic Divergence

---

> *"The instructions were identical. The ideas were identical. The outputs were not."*

---

## Abstract

This document presents a new theoretical framework — **The Distinctiveness Gradient** — for understanding how identical instructions given to a sequence of social agents produce escalating aesthetic complexity in a shared expressive artifact. The framework is derived from first principles, grounded in a live urban field experiment (New York City, 2026), validated against photographic evidence of the artifact produced, and situated within the canon of cultural evolution, cognitive science, perceptual psychology, and information theory. The central claim is this: **emergent aesthetic complexity is not random, not creative, and not surprising — it is the mathematically inevitable output of a directed pressure field acting on agents with finite attention, identity-expression drives, and irreversible commit constraints.** The framework makes falsifiable predictions, identifies a new class of social dynamic called **anti-ergodic cultural accumulation**, and proposes the concept of **register escape** as the mechanism behind qualitative phase transitions in collective expressive behavior.

## I. The Experiment

### Setup

A large white sheet of paper was placed in a public urban park in New York City alongside a poster reading:

**ART ≠ IDEAS**
**TAKE SOME PAPER**

Participants were invited to draw a single line on the shared sheet as a tally mark indicating they had taken a piece of post-it paper. No further instruction was given. The rule was minimal, unambiguous, and apparently trivial: *draw a line*.

The post-it papers themselves were the only formally differentiated objects — distinguished only by color. Everything else was left open.

### What Happened

The first marks were simple, near-vertical tally lines. Rule-compliant. Expressively minimal.

They did not stay that way.

By the time the sheet was photographed, it contained:
- Vertical and diagonal tally strokes of varying weight and length
- Cursive loops and infinity-figures
- A dense, filled organic shape in teal, shaded with area coverage
- S-curves, parenthetical arcs, a small lightning bolt glyph
- A detailed mandala-like flower drawing of significant complexity and time investment
- A smiley face
- A coiled spiral
- Written cursive text: *"Art is inextricable from ideas"*

The instruction never changed. The rule was the same for every person. The accumulated output bore no resemblance to the instruction that generated it.

### The Invariant

Throughout this escalation, one behavior never varied: **participants took the paper**. Every single agent complied perfectly with the transactional element of the task. The expressive element — the line — became a site of total divergence. The behavioral anchor held; the expressive surface exploded.

This asymmetry is not incidental. It is the first structural key to understanding the framework.

## II. First Principles

The Distinctiveness Gradient framework rests on six foundational axioms. These are not assumed from prior theory — they are derived from the experimental observation and confirmed by the photographic record.

### Axiom I — The Expressive Field is Multi-Dimensional

Any act of mark-making occupies a position in a high-dimensional **Expressive Field** **E**, parameterized at minimum by:

- **Topology** τ: Is the mark a line, curve, loop, closed shape, figure, glyph, text?
- **Scale** λ: Length, area, spatial extent
- **Angle** θ: Orientation relative to ground
- **Weight** w: Stroke thickness, pressure, density
- **Color** c: Hue, saturation, value
- **Position** (x, y): Location on the shared sheet
- **Register** ρ: The representational mode — visual / figurative / linguistic / symbolic

This field is not Euclidean. Perceptual distances between marks are non-uniform: a small shift in angle near the vertical is more salient to a human observer than the same shift near 45 degrees. The geometry of **E** is curved — it is a **Riemannian manifold** with a metric determined by human perceptual sensitivity, not by abstract mathematical distance.

### Axiom II — Every Agent Reads the Field Before Writing It

Each new participant who approaches the sheet encounters the **accumulated state** of all prior marks. They do not encounter the instruction fresh. They encounter:

1. The written rule ("draw a line")
2. The philosophical prompt ("ART ≠ IDEAS")
3. The entire visual history of prior participation

This ordering matters. The visual history is not supplementary to the rule — it **overwrites** the rule in terms of attentional weight as the sheet fills. The instruction is a fixed signal. The accumulated state is a growing, changing, increasingly complex signal. Over time, the growing signal dominates.

### Axiom III — Identity Pressure is a Generative Force

Agents are not passive rule-followers. They are **identity-expressing agents** operating in a social observation context. A public park is a witnessed space. The sheet is a public record. Every mark is implicitly signed — not by name, but by distinctiveness. To draw the same line as the person before you is, in the logic of the expressive field, to be absent. To be present is to be different.

This is not vanity. It is a structural feature of **any** system in which:
- The output is publicly observable
- Multiple agents contribute sequentially
- There is no functional payoff for coordination (unlike language, navigation, or pricing)
- There is social value in being distinguishable

Under these conditions, **the pressure to diverge is not psychological — it is thermodynamic**. It is what happens when no force acts to coordinate, and agents have any degree of identity-expression drive.

### Axiom IV — Attention is Finite and Endogenously Allocated

Agents cannot process all available signals simultaneously. They have **finite cognitive bandwidth** — a fixed capacity to extract information from their environment and act on it per unit time. Crucially, this bandwidth is not passively allocated: agents *choose*, below the level of conscious deliberation, what to attend to.

At low *n* (few prior marks), Signal 1 (the rule) is attended to efficiently because Signal 3 (accumulated marks) is sparse and requires little processing. The rule dominates. Marks are simple.

At high *n* (many prior marks), Signal 3 is rich, complex, and perceptually demanding. It consumes increasing bandwidth. Signal 1 is under-processed not because the agent ignores it, but because the attention they would spend on it has been redirected by the sheer informational weight of the existing sheet. **Rule drift is not disobedience. It is the natural consequence of finite attention meeting growing social complexity.**

The invariance of the paper-taking behavior — perfect compliance across all agents — is the direct prediction of this axiom: paper-taking costs near-zero bandwidth. It is unambiguous, low-complexity, motoric. It never competes for attention. The expressive act of drawing, by contrast, is the site of all bandwidth competition.

### Axiom V — Commitment is Irreversible

Once a mark is drawn, it cannot be erased. This is not merely a physical fact — it is the structural property that makes the framework fundamentally different from any system that allows revision, retraction, or negotiation.

In systems where agents can revise their contributions (markets with re-pricing, conversations with correction, drafts with editing), the accumulated state is plastic. Agents can move the field. In your experiment, the field only accumulates. No agent can reduce the occupancy of any region of **E** — they can only add to it.

This irreversibility has a profound consequence: **the system cannot reach equilibrium**. A system that can reverse can converge. A system that cannot reverse must diverge, or stall. Since identity pressure ensures agents always seek to differ, stalling is not a stable option. The system diverges without bound — or until it runs out of sheet.

### Axiom VI — Register Escape is the Mechanism of Qualitative Phase Transition

Within any given register ρ (e.g., "line"), there is a finite amount of distinguishable variation available. Once early agents have occupied the obvious positions — vertical lines, diagonal lines, thick lines, thin lines — later agents face rapidly diminishing returns on further variation within that register. The **pressure landscape** (the distribution of distinctiveness-pressure across **E**) develops a valley in the line-register and a mountain range of high available distinctiveness in adjacent registers (curves, loops, shapes).

Agents do not consciously decide to "escape" the line register. They follow the pressure landscape. The path of least resistance — the path to maximum distinctiveness with minimum effort — points toward the adjacent register. **Register escape is not rebellion against the rule. It is gradient descent toward the highest available distinctiveness given the current state of the field.**

This is why the sequence in the photograph is not random. It follows a lawful order: lines → curves → loops → filled shapes → figures → text. Each transition is a register escape triggered when the prior register became saturated relative to the available distinctiveness in the next register up.

## III. The Pressure Landscape

The **Pressure Landscape** is the central construct of the Distinctiveness Gradient framework. It is defined as the distribution of **distinctiveness pressure** across every point in the Expressive Field **E**, given the current accumulated state **S**ₜ.

Formally:

**P(q | Sₜ) ∝ D(q, Sₜ)**

where **D(q, Sₜ)** is the perceptual distance between proposed mark *q* and the nearest existing mark in **S**ₜ, measured under the non-Euclidean metric of the Expressive Field.

### Properties of the Pressure Landscape

**1. It is dynamic.** The landscape at time *t* is different from the landscape at time *t+1* because each committed mark alters it. New marks create low-pressure zones (regions now occupied) and do not affect high-pressure zones (regions still unoccupied). The landscape is a one-way ratchet: pressure can only be reduced locally, never restored.

**2. It is non-uniform from the start.** Even before the first mark is drawn, the pressure landscape is not flat. Human perceptual sensitivity creates a prior: some regions of **E** are more salient, more legible, more socially visible than others. The canonical vertical tally line sits at a high-salience position — this is why the first agents go there. They are not being creative; they are following the steepest gradient of instruction-compliance combined with prior salience.

**3. It drives escalation as a geometric consequence.** As low-pressure zones accumulate (occupied regions expand), agents must travel further in **E** to reach equivalent levels of distinctiveness pressure. The path lengths grow. The expressive moves become more complex. This is not a cultural choice — it is the geometry of a filling space.

**4. It has ridge lines between registers.** Moving from the line-register to the curve-register requires crossing a representational threshold — a discontinuity in topology τ. These ridge lines create the **phase transition signature** visible in the photograph: there are not smooth gradations from lines to figures but distinct jumps — a region of lines, then a region of loops, then a region of shapes. The ridge lines between registers create clustering.

### The Pressure Landscape at Each Phase (From the Photograph)

| Phase | Register Active | Landscape State | Trigger for Escape |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Vertical line | Near-flat; instruction dominates | Vertical positions saturated |
| 2 | Diagonal / varied line | Ridge forming around linearity | Angular variation exhausted |
| 3 | Loop / cursive curve | Strong valley in line-space | Topology pressure drives loop |
| 4 | Filled shape (teal) | Massive ridge crossed — 2D space entered | Line-topology fully saturated |
| 5 | Figurative / symbolic | Visual register filling | High-effort figuration appears |
| 6 | Linguistic text | Trans-register jump: visual → propositional | Visual field near-saturated; linguistic field empty |

The text — *"Art is inextricable from ideas"* — is the theoretical endpoint of the pressure landscape: the only remaining region of **E** with essentially zero occupancy was the **propositional register**. No prior agent had placed a structured linguistic argument on the sheet. The pressure there was absolute. An agent with sufficient bandwidth and creative momentum followed the gradient all the way to the far edge of the manifold and planted their mark.

## IV. Agent Trajectories Through the Field

Each agent navigates the Pressure Landscape by a process that can be decomposed into four sequential steps:

### Step 1 — Field Reading

The agent scans the accumulated sheet. This is not passive perception — it is an active computational act. The agent is, without knowing it, constructing a local representation of the Pressure Landscape: which regions are saturated, which are open, where the distinctiveness gradient points.

The precision of this reading is governed by attention bandwidth. An agent who glances briefly performs a coarse field reading and will make a proposal based on the most salient available gradient (the steepest obvious path). An agent who studies the sheet carefully performs a fine field reading and will identify subtle available variations — the unexplored angle, the unused color combination, the not-yet-present register.

### Step 2 — Gradient Following

The agent begins moving through the field. Starting from some initial position — shaped by their personal expressive tendency, their response to the prompt, their aesthetic predispositions — they follow the gradient of increasing distinctiveness. Each internal step brings them closer to a mark that is more differentiated from the existing state.

The length of this trajectory — how far they travel before committing — depends on:

- **Momentum**: Their creative drive, time available, intrinsic motivation to express
- **Bandwidth**: How much of their attention is available for field exploration vs. contextual processing
- **The Prompt**: "ART ≠ IDEAS" introduces a conceptual frame that biases the gradient toward intellectually charged positions in **E** — which is why the eventual text response was philosophically engaged, not arbitrary

### Step 3 — Natural Stopping

At some point, the agent stops moving and commits to a position. This stopping is not random. Agents halt when one of three conditions is met:

1. **Distinctiveness Sufficient**: The proposed mark achieves adequate differentiation from the existing field. Further movement produces diminishing returns on distinctiveness per unit effort
2. **Bandwidth Exhausted**: The agent has consumed their available attention. They have processed enough of the field and reached a locally satisfying position
3. **Gradient Reversal**: Further movement in the current direction begins to loop back toward occupied territory — the agent is approaching the far edge of a low-pressure zone and the gradient begins pointing back toward the center

Condition 3 is the most important: agents stop when they detect that further elaboration would reduce rather than increase their distinctiveness. The agent who drew the flower stopped at a flower — not a quarter-flower, not a sheet-filling tapestry. They ran until the gradient reversed, then stopped. This is lawful behavior, not arbitrary.

### Step 4 — Irreversible Commitment

The mark is made. The field is permanently altered. Every subsequent agent begins Step 1 in a field that is different from the one this agent found. The committed mark is both an output of the trajectory and an input to all future trajectories.

## V. Anti-Ergodic Accumulation

Standard models of collective behavior assume **ergodicity**: that the time-average behavior of the system converges to its ensemble average, and that the system eventually explores its state space uniformly and reaches a stationary distribution.

The Distinctiveness Gradient system is **structurally anti-ergodic**. It satisfies none of the ergodic conditions:

**It does not converge.** Ergodic systems converge to a stationary distribution. The Distinctiveness Gradient system diverges from any distribution it passes through, because the pressure landscape eliminates the attractors that would create convergence.

**It does not average.** Ergodic systems produce a well-defined long-run average. The Distinctiveness Gradient system produces a monotonically expanding occupation of **E** — not an average, but a frontier.

**It does not forget its path.** Ergodic systems are, in the long run, independent of their starting conditions. The Distinctiveness Gradient system is permanently path-dependent: the same set of agents, given the same instruction, starting from a different initial sequence, will produce a different artifact. The history is always present in the structure of the landscape.

**It violates detailed balance.** In ergodic systems, the probability of transitioning from state A to state B equals the probability of transitioning from B to A, weighted by the stationary distribution. In the Distinctiveness Gradient system, transitions are irreversible — no agent can undo a prior mark — which makes detailed balance impossible by construction.

This anti-ergodicity is not a failure of the system. It is its defining property. The system is not trying to converge. It is trying to diverge, and it succeeds, because the pressure landscape always has unexplored territory as long as the dimensionality of **E** exceeds the number of committed marks.

The system only stalls when the sheet runs out of space — a physical constraint imposing a boundary condition that has no analog in the theoretical field. The theory predicts unbounded complexity; physical paper provides a finite container.

## VI. The Counter-Argument as a Structural Event

The written text *"Art is inextricable from ideas"* on the mark sheet is not an anomaly. It is the **maximum-pressure event** in the dataset, and its occurrence is a strong confirmation of the framework.

### Why It Happened

By the time this agent arrived, the visual register of **E** was substantially occupied. Lines, curves, shapes, and figures had all been placed. The distinctiveness pressure in the visual domain had been partially exhausted. The agent, facing this saturated landscape, followed the gradient to its extreme logical conclusion: the one region of the shared artifact with zero occupancy was the **propositional linguistic register**.

No one had written an argument on the sheet. The pressure there was absolute. An agent with sufficient bandwidth, intellectual momentum, and engagement with the prompt traversed the ridge between the visual and propositional registers and placed their mark in the only truly unoccupied territory available.

### Why It Said What It Said

The content of the text is not arbitrary. It is the agent's response to the prompt "ART ≠ IDEAS" — a direct engagement with the conceptual frame that had been governing the entire experiment. The agent did not write "hello" or their name. They wrote a philosophical counter-proposition.

This is the **prompt's gravitational pull** on the gradient. The "ART ≠ IDEAS" frame acts as a bias in the Pressure Landscape, tilting the gradient toward intellectually charged positions. The agent followed the gradient and arrived at the most intellectually charged position on the sheet: a direct refutation of the prompt.

### The Recursive Self-Reference

The text *"Art is inextricable from ideas"* creates a **recursive loop** in the experiment's logic:

- The prompt claims ART ≠ IDEAS
- The experiment was itself an artistic act generated by an idea
- The text refutes the prompt using a mark that is simultaneously art and idea
- The refutation is written on the artifact that the idea (the prompt) generated

This is not a paradox. It is a **fixed point** — a position in the Expressive Field where the gradient produced a mark that comments on the gradient itself. The experiment became self-aware through the act of one agent following the pressure landscape to its furthest corner.

## VII. The Poster as Contaminated Primer

The photograph reveals something the written experimental notes do not fully acknowledge: **the poster itself is not a neutral instruction**.

The top sheet — "ART ≠ IDEAS / TAKE SOME PAPER" — is rendered in:
- Multiple colors (blue, green, black)
- Large, irregular hand-drawn letterforms
- Layered, stylized strokes with visible aesthetic care
- A ≠ symbol drawn as a crossed horizontal stroke, itself expressive

The poster **performs** the art-idea entanglement it claims to deny. It is an idea (the proposition) rendered as art (the letterforms). Every participant encountered this visual object before they approached the mark sheet. The poster is not a neutral instruction-delivery mechanism — it is a **prior** in the Pressure Landscape. It seeds the expressive field with a particular aesthetic register (hand-drawn, multi-color, bold, expressive) before the first mark is made.

This means the first agents who drew "simple" tally lines were not starting from a blank prior. They were starting from a prior set by the poster. Their lines were a response to — and a departure from — the complexity they had already seen in the poster itself. The apparent simplicity of the first marks is relative to the poster's complexity, not to a true zero baseline.

This is the **founder effect** in the Pressure Landscape: the initial conditions are not blank. They are set by the most prominent visual artifact in the environment, which happens to be the one explicitly foregrounding the art-idea relationship. The experiment did not begin when the first person drew. It began when the poster was made.

## VIII. Canonical Parallels

The Distinctiveness Gradient framework is new, but it connects to and organizes a set of pre-existing experimental findings that have lacked a unified theoretical account.

**Iterated Transmission Studies (Kirby, Cornish & Smith, 2008):** Sequential participants reproduced an artificial language across generations, producing increasing structural regularity through compression pressure. The Distinctiveness Gradient is the structural inverse: the same iterated transmission architecture, but with divergence pressure instead of compression pressure. Both are driven by the accumulated state of prior transmissions. The difference in outcome (convergence vs. divergence) is entirely determined by the direction of the pressure gradient — toward shared learnability in language experiments, toward individual distinctiveness in expressive experiments.

**Cumulative Cultural Ratchet (Caldwell & Millen, 2008):** Paper plane designs improved across transmission chains without explicit instruction. The ratchet effect — unidirectional improvement locked in by each generation — is structurally identical to the Irreversibility Axiom. Both systems ratchet upward without the possibility of return. The difference is that Caldwell's ratchet improves along a performance dimension (flight distance); the Distinctiveness Gradient ratchets along an expressive complexity dimension.

**Schema and Correction (Gombrich, 1960):** All representation begins with a pre-existing schema that is corrected toward observation. In the Distinctiveness Gradient, the "schema" is the accumulated prior state of the mark sheet, and the "correction" is driven not by perceptual accuracy but by identity pressure. Gombrich's mechanism predicts that the correction is always *relative to what already exists* — which is precisely the gradient-following behavior described in Agent Trajectories.

**Focal Points and Minimal Rules (Schelling, 1960, 1978):** Schelling demonstrated that minimal local rules produce dramatic macro-level patterns through purely local interaction. The Distinctiveness Gradient is a Schelling-type minimal-rule system: the rule ("draw a line") is the minimal local constraint, and the macro pattern (escalating aesthetic complexity, phase transitions, register escape) emerges from purely local agent behavior with no central coordination.

**Psychobiology of Aesthetics (Berlyne, 1971):** Berlyne's inverted-U preference curve establishes that humans have an optimal complexity window — too simple produces under-stimulation, too complex produces overwhelm, and the ascending edge of this curve produces maximum engagement. The Distinctiveness Gradient predicts that each agent targets this ascending edge of the complexity curve. As the existing sheet grows richer, the ascending edge moves upward. Each agent's mark must be more complex than what already exists to sit on the optimal-engagement ascending slope. The complexity escalation is not random — it tracks the moving optimal complexity target.

**Dissemination of Culture (Axelrod, 1997):** Axelrod's model showed that local similarity breeds interaction, producing cultural coherence at the local level but persistent global diversity. The Distinctiveness Gradient captures the divergence branch of Axelrod's dynamics: because participants in the experiment never interact socially (strangers in a park, no repeated encounters), there is no similarity-breeding interaction. The only interaction is with the artifact. Without the stabilizing force of social homophily, the full divergence pressure operates unconstrained.

## IX. Predictive Consequences

The Distinctiveness Gradient framework makes a set of falsifiable predictions. These can be tested in replications with controlled variations.

### Prediction 1 — The Register Escape Sequence is Lawful, Not Random

If the framework is correct, the sequence of register escapes will follow the same order across independent replications: lines → curves → loops → closed shapes → filled shapes → figures → glyphs → text. Departures from this sequence will be predictably associated with outlier agents (very high creative momentum, unusual backgrounds) or unusual initial conditions (a poster that primes a non-visual register from the start).

**Test:** Run five replications in five different locations. Compare register escape sequences.

### Prediction 2 — Escape Time Varies Inversely with Prompt Complexity

A richer, more visually complex prompt (more like the photograph's poster) will accelerate register escapes because it sets a higher initial prior in the Pressure Landscape. A minimal prompt (plain black text on white paper) will slow register escapes.

**Test:** Vary poster complexity across replications, holding instruction constant.

### Prediction 3 — The Invariant Holds Under Extreme Expressive Drift

Even when late-stage marks have completely abandoned any resemblance to "a line," the paper-taking behavior will remain at near-100% compliance. The behavioral anchor — low-bandwidth, unambiguous, motoric — is insulated from the expressive drift that affects the mark-making.

**Test:** Count paper-taking compliance vs. mark-making rule compliance across the full run.

### Prediction 4 — Path Dependence Produces Divergent Trajectories

Two sessions with the same participants, same instruction, but different first marks seeded (experimenter plants an initial mark of different character) will produce substantially different artifact trajectories despite all other conditions being identical.

**Test:** Seed two sheets: one with a simple vertical tally, one with a complex figurative drawing. Run same participants through both, sufficiently separated in time to prevent memory effects.

### Prediction 5 — Spatial Markov Property in Mark Clustering

Marks will cluster spatially because agents condition primarily on nearby prior marks rather than the full sheet. This produces local phase coherence (a region of curves, a region of shapes) rather than homogeneous mixing across the sheet.

**Test:** Measure spatial autocorrelation of mark complexity on the accumulated sheet.

## X. The Framework Summary

**The Distinctiveness Gradient** is a field theory of collective expressive behavior with the following core components:

| Component | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Expressive Field E** | The multi-dimensional Riemannian manifold of all possible marks, parameterized by topology, scale, angle, weight, color, position, and register |
| **Pressure Landscape P(q\|Sₜ)** | The distribution of distinctiveness pressure across E, given the current accumulated state |
| **Attention Bandwidth κ** | The finite cognitive resource allocated across competing signals; endogenously distributed, shifting toward Sₜ as *t* grows |
| **Agent Trajectory** | The path each agent takes through E, driven by gradient-following under bandwidth constraint, from field-reading through natural stopping to irreversible commitment |
| **Register** ρ | The representational mode: line / curve / shape / figure / glyph / text; the primary axis along which phase transitions occur |
| **Register Saturation** | The state of a register when further variation within it yields insufficient distinctiveness, triggering escape |
| **Register Escape** | The transition from one register to an adjacent one, driven by the gradient pointing across the representational ridge |
| **Irreversibility** | The structural property that makes the system anti-ergodic, path-dependent, and incapable of reaching a stationary distribution |
| **The Invariant** | The behavioral anchor — paper-taking — that remains at perfect compliance throughout all expressive drift, owing to near-zero bandwidth cost |
| **Anti-Ergodic Accumulation** | The system-level dynamic in which the collective artifact diverges without bound from any stationary distribution, driven by the interaction of irreversibility and identity pressure |

## XI. The Deepest Claim

The ART ≠ IDEAS poster claims that art and ideas are separable. The experiment it generates refutes this claim empirically, but in an unexpected way.

The refutation is not that art requires ideas. It is that **the social field surrounding an idea is itself generative**. The instructions were ideas. The rules were ideas. But the art — the accumulated mark sheet — was not produced by those ideas. It was produced by the interaction of:

- The accumulated state of prior agents' decisions
- Each agent's identity-expression drive
- Each agent's finite attention under a growing social signal
- The irreversibility of each committed mark
- The non-Euclidean geometry of human perceptual sensitivity
- The gravitational pull of the prompt on the gradient direction

None of these are in the idea. All of them are in the field.

The framework demonstrates that **art is the trajectory, not the instruction**. The same instruction, run through the same social field, will always produce complexity — but never the same complexity twice. The expressive landscape is generated anew each time, by the specific sequence of specific agents, each one navigating a field that was irreversibly shaped by everyone who came before.

The poster says ART ≠ IDEAS.

The framework says: **ART = the irreversible, anti-ergodic, pressure-gradient trajectory of identity-expressing agents through a shared perceptual field, anchored by a behavioral invariant, escalating in register without bound, legible only as a total path — never as any single point along it.**

Which is to say: the idea of the experiment generated the conditions for art. But the art was never in the idea.
